Yes — geography changes the reading. At the individual-sale level the relationship is statistically significant but tiny (r ≈ 0.12, driven mostly by sample size). Aggregated up to census tracts, it disappears entirely (p ≈ 0.12 — not significant; tracts are simply too large and too heterogeneous internally to show a location effect). Re-aggregated to ~0.46 km hexbins, a real relationship reappears: r ≈ 0.21 (p < .001), roughly 70% stronger than the record-level correlation, and it holds up whether the hex is small or larger — see note below. That means there is a genuine, geographically-clustered tendency for higher-$/sqft pockets to also take longer to sell, but census tracts are the wrong grain to see it: they average together distinct pricing submarkets that a finer hex grid keeps separate.
Robustness check across hex sizes (n≥5 cells only): resolution 7 (≈1.2 km, 141 cells) r=0.18; resolution 8 (≈0.46 km, 490 cells) r=0.21; resolution 9 (≈0.17 km, 686 cells) r=0.28 — all significant at p<.05. The effect strengthens, if anything, at finer resolution — not an artifact of one particular hex size.

"Fast" = at or below the countywide median of 74 days on market; "pricey" = at or above the countywide median of $157.82/sqft, both measured on hex medians. The two consistent-with-the-trend quadrants (fast+affordable and slow+pricey) together account for — of sales — noticeably more than the two quadrants that run against it (fast+pricey and slow+affordable, — combined). That's the shape behind the r ≈ 0.21 hex-level correlation above: a real but middling tendency, not a hard rule.
